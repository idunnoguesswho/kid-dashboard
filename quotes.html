🌟 B · Kid-Friendly Fun Fact
Octopuses Have Three Hearts! 🐙

An octopus has three hearts — two pump blood to its gills, and one pumps it to the rest of its body. But here's the wild part: when an octopus swims, the heart that pumps blood to the body actually stops beating. That's why octopuses prefer crawling over swimming — they literally tire themselves out with their own hearts!

Oh, and their blood is blue because it uses copper instead of iron. No wonder they're basically aliens of the sea. 🌊

📚 C · Merriam-Webster Word of the Day
fawn · verb · \FAWN\

To fawn means to seek someone's approval through excessive praise, flattery, or special attention — especially directed at someone important or powerful. It can also mean to show affection, particularly of dogs.

"Still new to celebrity, the musician blushed at the restaurant staff fawning over her during her hometown visit."
Etymology: From Old English fægen or fagan ("glad"), via fagnian ("to rejoice"). Interestingly, this fawn is completely unrelated to the fawn meaning a baby deer — that one comes from Latin fetus ("offspring"). Same spelling, totally different family tree!
🏛️ D · This Day in History — April 2
1513
Juan Ponce de León lands in Florida — The Spanish explorer became the first known European to set foot on what is now the U.S. state of Florida, landing somewhere near modern-day St. Augustine. He named it La Pascua Florida ("Feast of Flowers") after the Easter celebration.
1792
U.S. Mint established — Congress passed the Coinage Act, creating the United States Mint and officially establishing the dollar as the currency of the new nation. Before this, Americans mostly bartered or used foreign coins.
1800
Beethoven premieres his First Symphony — Ludwig van Beethoven conducted the premiere of Symphony No. 1 in Vienna. Critics loved it, and it launched one of history's most celebrated composing careers — though his greatest works were still to come.
1902
First full-time movie theater opens — The "Electric Theatre" opened in Los Angeles, becoming the first permanent movie theater in the United States. Admission was 10 cents. Cinema as we know it was born.
1968
2001: A Space Odyssey premieres — Stanley Kubrick's landmark sci-fi film had its world premiere in Washington, D.C. It polarized audiences at first, but is now considered one of the greatest films ever made and set the standard for science fiction cinema.
1982
Argentina invades the Falkland Islands — Argentine forces seized the British-held South Atlantic islands, triggering the Falklands War. Britain sent a naval task force and retook them 74 days later. The war had lasting political consequences for both nations.
✝️ E · Bible Verse of the Day
John 16:33 (ESV) — YouVersion Verse of the Day
"I have said these things to you, that in me you may have peace. In the world you will have tribulation. But take heart; I have overcome the world."

Personal application: Jesus speaks these words the night before the cross — in the middle of chaos, He offers peace. Today, when the weather is gloomy, the task list is long, and the meeting runs late, this is an invitation to exhale. Tribulation is real, but it isn't the final word. You can walk through hard things without being destroyed by them.

Context: These words come from the Farewell Discourse — Jesus' final teaching to His disciples before His arrest. He is preparing them (and us) for the reality that following Him wouldn't mean an easy life, but would mean living with a peace that surpasses circumstances. The timing in Holy Week makes this verse especially poignant — the cross is near, yet He says "take heart."

⚖️ H · Marcus Aurelius / Daily Stoic Reflection
On the Power of the Mind
"You have power over your mind — not outside events. Realize this, and you will find strength."
— Marcus Aurelius, Meditations

What Marcus meant: As Roman Emperor, Aurelius faced constant chaos — wars, plagues, political intrigue, the deaths of children. Yet he kept returning to this idea: we cannot govern what happens to us, only how we interpret and respond to it. The Stoics called this the dichotomy of control — the most liberating idea in philosophy.

Applied today: The forecast calls for snow and a packed schedule. The consolation check may be pending. None of that is in your control. What is in your control: how you begin the morning, what you choose to focus on, how kindly you treat yourself when things go sideways.

Practice for today: Before the first meeting, take one slow breath and name one thing you are grateful for right now.

"Demands made upon other people for too much attention, protection, and love can only invite domination or revulsion."

Today's AA reflection is titled Character Building. It invites us to look honestly at the ways we may lean too heavily on others to fill what only our own inner work can satisfy. 🧠 J · Britannica Encyclopedia Fact
The Battle of Copenhagen & Nelson's Blind Eye 🔭

On April 2, 1801, British Admiral Horatio Nelson fought the Battle of Copenhagen against a Danish-Norwegian fleet. At a critical moment, his commander signaled him to disengage — to stop the fight. Nelson famously held the telescope to his blind eye and said, "I really do not see the signal." He continued fighting and won decisively.

The phrase "turning a blind eye" — meaning to deliberately ignore something — entered the English language from this moment. Nelson's selective obedience saved the battle. It also created one of history's most useful idioms, used everywhere from offices to parenting situations to this very day.
